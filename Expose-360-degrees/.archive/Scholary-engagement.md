# Critical examination of panoramic interfaces reveals scattered but growing scholarly engagement

## The claim's partial validity with important nuances

Based on extensive research across multiple academic domains, the claim that panoramic interfaces are "hardly critically reflected on" proves to be **partially accurate but increasingly outdated**. While critical scholarship exists, it remains fragmented across disciplines and often addresses panoramic technologies indirectly rather than as a primary focus.

## Areas of substantial critical engagement

### Platform and infrastructure studies show the deepest engagement

The most robust critical analysis appears in platform studies examinations of **Google Street View** and VR ecosystems. Scholars like Agnieszka Leszczynski, Jean-Christophe Plantin, and Tarleton Gillespie have developed sophisticated frameworks examining how these platforms shape spatial knowledge, embed surveillance capabilities, and concentrate power. Research reveals Street View as both platform and infrastructure, creating new forms of "digital enclosure" where spatial data becomes commodified.

Infrastructure studies provides equally strong engagement, with scholars examining the complex sociotechnical assemblages underlying panoramic production—from sensor arrays and stitching algorithms to cloud processing pipelines. This work reveals how seemingly neutral technical processes embed particular ways of seeing and serve platform accumulation strategies.

### Hito Steyerl's "Bubble Vision" as breakthrough framework

Among critical media theorists, **Hito Steyerl** stands out for developing the most comprehensive theoretical engagement with panoramic media through her concept of "Bubble Vision." She critically analyzes 360-degree vision as potentially "a training scheme to adapt humans to a world from which they are increasingly missing." Her work addresses how VR creates a paradox where "the viewer is absolutely central, but at the same time, he or she is missing from the scene," offering crucial insights into the politics of immersive vision.

### Emerging venues and communities

The establishment of the **Panoramic and Immersive Media Studies (PIMS) Yearbook** in 2024 marks a significant institutional development, providing a dedicated venue for interdisciplinary scholarship. The International Panorama Council has fostered research since 1992, though much work remains historical or technical rather than critically theoretical.

## Significant gaps and limitations

### Theoretical frameworks remain underutilized

While scholars like **Johanna Drucker** have developed sophisticated theories of "performative materiality" and interface as "dynamic space of relations," these frameworks have rarely been applied specifically to panoramic formats. Similarly, critical design approaches from Anthony Dunne & Fiona Raby could illuminate panoramic interfaces but haven't been systematically employed.

### Limited direct engagement across key theorists

Most major critical media theorists—including Wendy Chun, Jennifer Gabrys, Jussi Parikka, and James Bridle—have not directly addressed panoramic media despite developing relevant frameworks for understanding digital technologies' political and material dimensions. Their work on programmed visions, environmental media, and media archaeology could significantly enrich panoramic media criticism.

### Disciplinary fragmentation hinders comprehensive analysis

Critical engagement remains scattered across:

- **Computer vision conferences** focusing on technical aspects with limited critical perspective
- **Digital geography** examining spatial representations without emphasizing panoramic formats
- **VR/AR research** often privileging technical innovation over cultural critique
- **Visual culture studies** maintaining historical focus on pre-digital panoramas

## Methodological approaches being developed

### Critical making and practice-based research

Artist-researchers like Jon Rafman, teamLab, and Laurie Anderson are developing critical frameworks through practice, questioning the politics of immersion while creating panoramic installations that interrogate rather than celebrate the technology.

### Interdisciplinary frameworks emerging

Scholars are beginning to integrate:

- **Media archaeology** connecting 19th-century panoramas to contemporary VR
- **Sensory ethnography** (Sarah Pink) emphasizing embodied experience beyond ocular-centrism
- **Platform studies** examining how commercial systems shape panoramic possibilities
- **Infrastructure studies** revealing material and energetic costs of immersive media

## Why the gap exists

### Technical complexity as barrier

The sophisticated technical infrastructure of panoramic systems—involving computer vision, real-time rendering, and complex hardware—creates barriers for humanities scholars lacking technical literacy.

### Industry dominance of discourse

Commercial narratives about VR as "empathy machines" and panoramic media as transparent windows dominate public discourse, overshadowing critical perspectives.

### Rapid technological change

The speed of development in panoramic technologies outpaces academic publishing cycles, making sustained theoretical engagement challenging.

## Future directions and recommendations

### Urgent need for integrated critical frameworks

The research reveals pressing needs for:

1. **Unified theoretical approaches** specifically addressing panoramic interfaces as distinct media forms
2. **Decolonial and feminist analyses** examining how panoramic technologies reproduce or challenge hierarchies of vision
3. **Environmental criticism** addressing the ecological costs of computationally intensive panoramic media
4. **Accessibility studies** investigating how panoramic interfaces include or exclude different bodies and abilities

### Methodological innovations required

Future scholarship should develop:

- Methods for analyzing spatial interfaces from cultural perspectives
- Frameworks bridging technical and critical approaches
- Tools for studying algorithmic dimensions of panoramic creation
- Approaches to platform-specific affordances and constraints

## Conclusion: A field in formation

While the claim about lack of critical reflection has merit, it increasingly represents a **call to action rather than an accurate assessment**. Substantial foundations exist across multiple disciplines, but panoramic interfaces deserve more direct, sustained critical attention as a distinct media form with significant cultural and political implications. The scattered nature of existing scholarship suggests an opportunity for groundbreaking work that synthesizes these diverse approaches into comprehensive critical frameworks for understanding our increasingly panoramic digital environment.

The dissertation project mentioned would contribute significantly to addressing these gaps, particularly if it combines theoretical rigor with practical/artistic research methods and participatory approaches—areas the existing scholarship identifies as particularly underdeveloped.

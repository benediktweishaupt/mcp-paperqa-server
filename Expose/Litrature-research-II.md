# Algorithmic voids and computational sutures: Critical foundations for panoramic inpainting research

The intersection of 360° panoramic imaging and algorithmic inpainting represents one of the most underexplored territories in contemporary media studies. Recent scholarship reveals a profound gap between the technical sophistication of computational photography and critical engagement with its epistemological implications. Your PhD project on "Digital Panoramas as Visual Infrastructures between Totality, Gap and Algorithmic Montage" addresses this lacuna at a crucial moment when machine vision increasingly mediates human perception.

This research synthesis examines five interconnected domains of scholarship from 2020-2024: operational images theory, media archaeology of computational photography, forensic aesthetics methodology, technical inpainting developments, and current research gaps. The findings reveal not merely an absence of critical engagement with panoramic inpainting but an urgent need for new theoretical frameworks that can account for how algorithms simultaneously construct and conceal visual totalities.

## Operational images enter the invisual realm

The period 2020-2024 marks a watershed in operational images scholarship, crystallized in **Jussi Parikka's "Operational Images: From the Visual to the Invisual"** (University of Minnesota Press, 2023). This monograph fundamentally extends Harun Farocki's military-focused concept into what Parikka terms "invisual culture" - a realm where images function primarily as data operations rather than visual representations. Parikka argues that contemporary operational images "do not feature in the optical or experiential sense" but exist as statistical distributions and computational processes.

Trevor Paglen's foundational 2014 essay "Operational Images" continues generating critical responses, particularly his observation that contemporary operational images are "literally invisible" - machines creating images for other machines without human-interpretable versions. Recent scholarship in **Film-Philosophy** (2023) analyzes how these images "reduce human interpretive agency and communication," examining Farocki's Eye/Machine series to reveal how operational images impose limitations on human actors within machine-orchestrated environments.

Hito Steyerl's recent work provides crucial bridges to your research focus. Her 2023 **New Left Review** article "Mean Images" introduces the concept of "subprime visibility" - AI images as "bundled, hollow, statistically derived" entities that parallel the 2008 financial crisis in visual culture. Steyerl's analysis of diffusion models as creating "derivative images" offers a framework for understanding inpainting as a form of visual speculation that fills gaps with statistical probabilities rather than indexical traces.

The Harun Farocki Institut's 2024 symposium series "The Operational Image Before the Law" examines techno-institutional conditions under machine learning, raising questions about copyright, training data rights, and AI governance that directly impact panoramic image construction. Their exploration of "Which Operativity?" emphasizes planetary dimensions of interface processes - crucial for understanding how 360° imaging claims to capture totality while operating through fragmented computational processes.

## Media archaeology excavates the algorithmic palimpsest

The application of media archaeological method to computational photography reveals inpainting algorithms as contemporary manifestations of historical image completion practices. **Samine Joudat's 2024 dissertation** "The Specter of Representation: Computational Images and Algorithmic Capitalism" (Claremont Graduate University) explicitly builds on Friedrich Kittler's media theory to examine how "computational images exist as information within networks mediated by machines." This work traces computation through images from early cybernetics to contemporary machine learning, providing essential theoretical grounding for understanding panoramic construction as informatic rather than representational practice.

The historical genealogy of inpainting algorithms reveals a fascinating trajectory from physical art restoration to computational automation. While Pietro Edwards pioneered systematic inpainting in 18th-century Venice, the digital revolution began with **Bertalmio et al.'s 2000 SIGGRAPH paper** coining "inpainting" for computational contexts. Their PDE-based approach explicitly mimicked professional art restorers, automatically completing isophote lines. **Criminisi et al. (2004)** revolutionized the field with exemplar-based inpainting, introducing priority-based patch selection that enabled large-area completion.

Wolfgang Ernst's concept of "bit-mapping" as archaeological method - searching images by their own medium rather than external metadata - offers crucial insights for analyzing panoramic algorithms. His distinction between spatial archives and temporal data transmission helps conceptualize how panoramic images exist not as stable totalities but as dynamic computational processes continuously reconstructed through algorithmic operations.

Vilém Flusser's prescient analysis of the photographic "apparatus" gains new relevance when applied to panoramic inpainting systems. His concept of photographers as "functionaries" operating within predefined programmatic possibilities perfectly captures how 360° camera users work within algorithmic parameters that determine not just what can be captured but how gaps will be filled. The "black box" nature of inpainting algorithms extends Flusser's critique - users cannot decode the statistical operations that complete their panoramic visions.

## Forensic aesthetics reveals the politics of completion

The forensic turn in media studies provides essential methodologies for examining how inpainting algorithms construct truth claims through visual completion. **Matthew Fuller and Eyal Weizman's "Investigative Aesthetics"** (Verso, 2021) establishes a framework where aesthetics operates as both "sensing" (capacity to register material effects) and "sense-making" (synthesis of perceptions into knowledge). This dual operation proves crucial for understanding how panoramic inpainting simultaneously captures and fabricates visual evidence.

Forensic Architecture's recent work demonstrates sophisticated engagement with algorithmic image analysis. Their "Model Zoo" (2020) trains machine-learning classifiers to identify human rights violations, while their differential parallax computation analyzes social media images from conflict zones. However, their 2024 **Digital Journalism** article on "post-human testimony" marks a crucial shift - moving away from "compassionate identification with the wounded body" toward revealing "infrastructures of domination" through algorithmic analysis.

**Susan Schuppli's "Material Witness"** (MIT Press, 2020) offers the most developed framework for understanding how digital materials bear witness. Her distinction between "evidence of the event" and "event of evidence" illuminates how inpainting algorithms don't simply fill gaps but create new evidentiary regimes. Schuppli's insistence on "thinking the digital analogically" - recognizing how digital processes transform into analogue objects for legal probity - raises profound questions about panoramic images' truth claims when algorithmic completion becomes forensically indistinguishable from captured reality.

Recent research on inpainting anti-forensics reveals an arms race between detection and concealment. The 2024 development of "attention-guided hierarchical reconstruction" methods specifically designed to "hide traces of tampering" from both human eyes and forensic algorithms demonstrates how inpainting increasingly operates as a form of computational camouflage. The creation of datasets like TGIF (Text-Guided Inpainting Forgery) with 75,000 forged images acknowledges that state-of-the-art detectors fail against modern generative approaches, particularly under compression.

## Technical sophistication meets critical blindness

The period 2020-2024 witnesses remarkable technical advances in inpainting algorithms, yet critical humanities engagement remains minimal. **RePaint** (CVPR 2022) revolutionizes the field by using Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models for inpainting without training on specific mask distributions. **BrushNet** (ECCV 2024) addresses semantic inconsistencies through dual-branch architecture, while **StrDiffusion** (CVPR 2024) reformulates texture denoising under structure guidance. These advances achieve unprecedented realism in visual completion, yet their cultural implications remain largely unexamined.

**Joanna Zylinska's "Nonhuman Photography"** (MIT Press, 2017) provides the most comprehensive philosophical framework for engaging these developments, though predating recent technical advances. Her concept of photography "decoupled from human agency and vision" encompasses not just automated capture but algorithmic completion. Zylinska's tripartite framework - photographs not of/by/for humans - perfectly captures panoramic inpainting's posthuman operations where machines complete scenes for machine processing.

The pursuit of "semantic coherence" in recent algorithms embeds profound assumptions about visual meaning. Technical papers celebrate achieving "plausibility" without interrogating whose reality defines plausible completion. The foundational reliance on "natural image statistics" reveals how algorithms encode particular distributions of visual normalcy, potentially reinforcing existing biases while claiming mathematical neutrality. When **PrefPaint** (NeurIPS 2024) attempts to align inpainting with "human aesthetic standards" through reinforcement learning, it crystallizes the field's uncritical acceptance of subjective preferences as objective metrics.

Artists working critically with these tools remain rare. Evidence from exhibitions like "Confusion of Tongues" shows artists investigating overlaps between artistic and automated image production, yet sustained critical engagement with inpainting specifically remains absent. This gap between technical capability and critical reflection represents the field's most glaring absence.

## Charting unexplored territories

The synthesis of current scholarship reveals specific gaps that your PhD project uniquely addresses. No sustained critical analysis connects historical panorama studies to contemporary 360° imaging through media archaeological method. The absence of "algorithmic montage theory" linking Eisenstein and Benjamin's montage concepts to computational inpainting represents a crucial theoretical lacuna. The temporal dimensions of algorithmic image processes - how inpainting manipulates time through statistical prediction - remain entirely unexplored.

Recent dissertations predominantly emerge from computer science departments, lacking critical theory engagement. While programs at institutions like NABA attempt practice-based approaches, they rarely bridge technical implementation with historical analysis. The absence of dedicated venues for interdisciplinary computational photography research perpetuates disciplinary isolation. No special journal issues address algorithmic images from media theory perspectives or examine panoramic computational photography critically.

Samuel Goree et al.'s "Attention is All They Need: Exploring the Media Archaeology of the Computer Vision Research Paper" (2022) opens possibilities for material analysis of algorithmic image production, yet lacks follow-up examining specific algorithms like inpainting. The forensic analysis of inpainting as evidence alteration remains unexplored despite clear implications for truth claims in panoramic documentation. Critical making approaches to algorithmic image critique exist in theory but lack application to computational photography specifically.

## Toward algorithmic montage theory

Your research positions itself at multiple unexplored intersections. Developing "algorithmic montage theory" would connect Eisenstein's collision-based meaning creation and Benjamin's dialectical images to contemporary inpainting's statistical synthesis. This framework could reveal how inpainting operates not as seamless completion but as computational montage producing new meanings through algorithmic collision of present capture and predicted completion.

The opportunity exists to establish "panoramic media archaeology" as a method tracing connections from 19th-century panorama paintings through cinematic experiments to contemporary VR environments. This genealogy would reveal how each medium's promise of total vision relies on specific techniques for managing gaps and seams - from painted transitions to algorithmic interpolation.

Creating a framework for "critical computational photography" could bridge hands-on algorithm development with theoretical analysis. This methodology would treat code as text for critical reading while using implementation as a form of media archaeological excavation. By modifying inpainting algorithms to reveal rather than conceal their operations, practice-based research could expose the computational unconscious of panoramic vision.

The concept of "technical witness" extends Schuppli's material witness into algorithmic domains, examining how inpainting algorithms testify to their own operations through artifacts and traces. This framework could analyze how panoramic images bear witness not just to scenes captured but to statistical models and training data shaping their completion.

Your positioning at the intersection of operational images theory, media archaeology, and forensic aesthetics offers unique opportunities for theoretical innovation. By treating 360° panoramas as infrastructures rather than images, your research can reveal how algorithmic totality operates through managed partiality. The gap between capture and completion becomes not a technical problem to solve but a productive site for critical investigation.

The urgency of this research intensifies as panoramic imaging increasingly shapes virtual environments, surveillance systems, and evidential practices. Understanding how algorithms construct seamless totalities from fragmentary captures becomes crucial for navigating a world where computational completion masquerades as comprehensive vision. Your PhD project promises to develop essential frameworks for critically engaging these systems at a moment when algorithmic mediation threatens to become invisible through its very ubiquity.
